1. Let's have a look at the big picture

Evolution of publications over time

This figure shows the rapid growth of economic literature on ecosystem services since the mid-2000s.

Source: Web of Science (search conducted in January 2026)

Since 1975, the number of publications on ecosystem services has increased dramatically: the first decade (1975–1984) had 33 articles, the next (1985–1994) 131, followed by 1,060 for 1995–2004, 13,711 for 2005–2014, and finally 66,309 for 2015–2024, showing an exponential growth of scientific interest in this topic.

2. Temporal evolution of clusters

Using VOSviewer, we can visualize the main clusters in ecosystem services research over time in terms of co-citations. We clearly observe two main communities: one developed in Europe and another in Asia.

The overlay visualization highlights a significant paradigm shift in ecosystem services research between 2016 and 2022. While the "foundational" period (shaded in dark blue and purple) was largely dominated by Western scholars, the more recent period (2020–2022, shaded in yellow) shows a massive surge in productivity from Asian institutions.

3. Temporal evolution of concepts

The overlay visualization highlights how the vocabulary evolved from biodiversity and conservation to valuation and market-based approaches.

The earliest journals publishing on ecosystem services (among those with at least 50 publications on this topic) were Science (1971) and Nature (1973). Over the period, 268 journals published at least 50 articles on ecosystem services. From the mid-1970s onward, more specialized journals began publishing extensively such as Biological Conservation (1973) and Environmental Management (1977).
